Introduction to GNU/Linux

"Those who do not understand UNIX
are condemned to reinvent it, poorly."
Henry Spence, Programmer

An operating system is a piece of software which facilitates the use of a computer. To this end, its two main responsibilities are to support running programs and to maintain persistent data. GNU/Linux belongs to a large class of operating systems known as UNIX-like, having an architecture, design philosophy, and system-call interface similar to the 70's-era operating system.

History:

Time Sharing

To understand Unix and the origins of GNU/Linux, we have to step back in time, to the 1960's, when the computer industry was just beginning. Computers then were immense— and largely uncontrolled— machines, either processing instructions from punched cards in discrete sessions called batches, or supporting some lucky user for an interactive session.[1]

Next-generation computers were interactive, their use being, "...indescribably more pleasant and productive than batch processing."[2] In 1960, an operating system was devised to "steal" time from batch jobs on behalf of interactive users, and thereby support both interactive and batch computing simultaneously.[1] In 1961, a purely time-sharing operating system— which could support four users simultaneously— was demonstrated.[1]

Computing's commercial future was widely believed to be in offering an information utility: In a business model similar to telephone or electricity, customers would connect their keyboards and monitors to a central mainframe, then pay for computation on an as-used basis.[3] Such "dial-up timesharing" presented a number of technical challenges, chief among them,

  1. Creating the necessary infrastructure to distribute the service
  2. The procurement of capable computer hardware
  3. Allotting useful and controlled computation to individual customers

AT&T, which dominated the country's telephone system, was uniquely positioned to address the first; General Electric was willing to produce the necessary hardware; and, MULTICS, an operating system project that began in 1965, intended to solve the third.

Multics

The MULTICS project was born at MIT, and its development was supplemented by GE and AT&T.[4] To address the challenges at hand, a number of novel features were introduced, many of which would become standard in computing.

The Envisioned Hardware

To support the controlled execution of customers' software, for example, it relied on the process, a virtualised environment within which software could safely be executed.[5] To support multiple users, multiple processes were supported simultaneously.[6] To manage these, a "supervisor" program was introduced.

To implement these ideas, Multics was written in a high-level programming language, PL/I.[6][7] Even by today's standards, PL/I was sophisticated: In order to future-proof the work, it was entirely platform-independent— it did not deal in what we would call "primitives."[8] As the project got underway, obtaining a compiler for it became a significant challenge, so much so that a lesser dialect was first devised and implemented.[7]

Although Multics was working by 1968, it could only support a small team of developers, and as it became increasingly clear that the project would not deliver on its promise of supporting a profitable information utility, AT&T withdrew.[2] Multics soldiered on: 1970 saw its first installation, which could support 35 simultaneous users. One of its later installations (General Motors) could support 200.[9]

Unix

Aside:

Bell Laboratories was AT&T's research arm.

In the wake of AT&T's withdrawal from Multics, operating systems researchers working at Bell Labs found themselves without a clear project.[10] Still reeling from the disaster of Multics, management refused to purchase any new computer equipment. For the researchers of Bell Labs, this period was frustrating: Their proposals, "...were never clearly and finally turned down, but yet were certainly never accepted."[10]

After finding a disused PDP-7, Ken Thompson and Dennis Ritchie decided to rewrite Space Travel for it.[10] Afterwards, Ken Thompson continued to work with the machine, implementing a filesystem that he, Dennis Ritchie and Rudd Canaday had devised on paper.[10]

In mid-1969, Thompson realized that he was close to an operating system: All he needed was an assembler, a text editor, a shell and a system call to launch programs.[2][10] The system call was "trivial", and the other three components were done in one week each.[2]

By late 1969, the software was self-supporting, and was quickly developed into a multi-user, time-sharing operating system, called Unix. Whereas Multics required specialized mainframe hardware, Unix made use of an emerging line of minicomputers. Whereas Multics was large, and offered many ways to handle a given task, Unix was simple, and usually offered one.

About his colleague's early work, Dennis Ritchie would later write,

"Thompson wanted to create a comfortable computing environment constructed according to his own design, using whatever means were available. His plans, it is evident in retrospect, incorporated many of the innovative aspects of Multics, including an explicit notion of a process as a locus of control, a tree-structured file system, a command interpreter as user-level program, simple representation of text files, and generalized access to devices."[11]

By offering text processing services for the purposes of patent applications within the laboratory, the team acquired a new PDP-11.[2] They supported a sequence of versions of the software and an accompanying online manual, while they themselves worked on "Research Unix."[2] By 1974, there were over fifty installations, with "...many more expected."[12] The first fork, BSD, was produced at the University of California, Berkeley, in 1977.[13]

The Unix Architecture

Unix had a simple, clear, well-documented architecture that inspired many operating systems. It is organized as a hierarchy of layers, with each layer building upon the ones beneath it, and supporting those above it:

Hardware: The core of any computer is its hardware. Each piece of hardware has a software interface defined by the engineers that created it; these interfaces are varied at best.

Drivers: Drivers interact with hardware, and simplify its interface for the kernel. Drivers are the deepest and most remote elements of operating systems, and are constantly being introduced as new hardware becomes available.

Kernel: The heart of the Unix operating system is its kernel. The kernel is responsible for scheduling the CPU, allotting memory, maintaining file integrity, and many other tasks. It is an involved piece of software— effectively, the "brains" of the operation.

System Calls: The set of operations that an operating system is willing to undertake is defined by the system calls it supports. The Unix architecture is characterized by six essential system calls:[14] fork(), exec(), open(), close(), read(), and write(). Both fork() and exec() are concerned with launching programs, while the other four deal with files.

Processes: A process is an executing program. Within the Unix family of operating systems, processes run the show: The kernel depends entirely on processes to tell it what to do, and takes no action without explicit instruction by a process.

GNU's Not Unix

Richard Stallman

The first attempt at producing a deliberately free and open-source Unix clone was announced by Richard Stallman in 1983. Stallman had been working at MIT's AI Laboratory for over a decade, and had grown accustomed to its culture of sharing source code:

"If you saw someone using an unfamiliar and interesting program, you could always ask to see the source code, so that you could read it, change it, or cannibalize parts of it to make a new program."[15]

Yet, proprietary software was becoming the norm, with students working on projects intended for profit, and companies going so far as to require nondisclosure agreements be signed to get an executable.[15][16] On September 27, 1983, Stallman made his announcement: To produce a "complete UNIX-compatible software system," and to "give it away free."[17] He called the project GNU, a recursive acronym for GNU's Not UNIX.[18]

Software Freedom

At the time of announcement, Stallman had not yet quantified his own intentions behind the word free. Certainly, "a free copy" did not embody the culture of code-sharing MIT was once home to. More, he did not wish to put the code in the public domain because modified versions of it it could become proprietary.[19] By 1985, Stallman had sorted these issues out:

Everyone will be permitted to modify and redistribute GNU, but no distributor will be allowed to restrict its further redistribution. That is to say, proprietary modifications will not be allowed. I want to make sure that all versions of GNU remain free.[20]

Thus, the notion of free software was born: Users can run, study, modify, copy and distribute GNU, but if they distribute, then those freedoms must be preserved. These stipulations are formalized in the GNU General Public License, now in its third version.

To create an entire operating system is an enormous task, but by 1990, the GNU Project had found or written all major components except one, the kernel.[18] Today, the GNU Project is well-regarded for producing high-quality, well-documented free software; they are as "official" as the open-source community gets. They remain active and prominent among the free software community, with monetary support from the Free Software Foundation.[22] Richard Stallman remains Chief GNUisance.[22]

The Linux Kernel

On August 25, 1991, a Finnish graduate student by the name of Linus Torvalds announced his own operating system project. Again following the Unix architecture, his project was intended to be "...just a hobby, won't be big and professional like GNU."[23]

Whereas GNU sought to produce an entire operating system, Linus restricted his attention to the kernel. By October of 1991, GNU's Bash, GCC, Make and other programs were running on his creation.[23] That month, he sent out the following invitation, which has since become famous:

No more all- nighters to get a nifty program working? Then this post might be just for you :-)
As I mentioned a month(?) ago, I'm working on a free version of a minix-lookalike for AT-386 computers. It has finally reached the stage where it's even usable (though may not be depending on what you want), and I am willing to put out the sources for wider distribution.

Aside:

In view of the "Bazaar" model's overwhelming dominance in modern open-source, Eric Raymond's paper is, in this author's opinion at least, no longer relevant.

Interest flourished, in large part because of the informality of the project: Everybody was invited, if only to tinker. His development model— "release early and often, delegate everything you can, be open to the point of promiscuity"[24]— raised eyebrows at the time, but has proved sound in virtue of its success: It now dominates the open-source landscape, with most projects following this, rather than the older "Cathedral" model.

The kernel is released under the GNU General Public License, and Linus Torvalds remains the maintainer of the Development branch. He often remarks on the kernel's latest releases. A major figure of today's Linux development effort is Greg Kroah-Hartman, who is lead maintainer of the stable branch, in addition to various subsystems.[25] According to him, interest in the kernel continues to grow, with 1200 new developers contributing each year.[26] Their main development bottlenecks are code review and maintainership.[26]

Linux Distributions:

Early History

The Linux kernel is made available by its developers in source-code form, and requires compilation on a separate host machine before it can be used. In addition, no other software is included, so that creating a functional Linux-based machine requires somebody to obtain, compile and assemble a number of disparate software components. A distribution is a project or community that fills this gap.

HJ Lu's Boot/Root Floppies

The very first distribution of GNU/Linux was undertaken by HJ Lu in 1992.[27] He packaged utilities alongside the kernel and distributed the collection on a pair of 5.25" disks. Known as the Boot/Root Floppies, it was never widely known.[27][28]

Linux's first widely used distribution was, ironically, a commercial venture, Soft Landing System. Released in late 1992, it was sold under the slogan "Gentle Touchdowns for DOS Bailouts,"[29] but users complained that the software was buggy,[28][30] and when its developers decided to change the executable file format, began to leave.[28]

Slackware

In late 1992, Patrick Volkerding needed a LISP interpreter for a project at school, and, having heard that clisp ran on SLS Linux, downloaded them both.[30] His professor got wind, and asked Patrick to help him install the same software on school computers.[30] Volkerding walked him through installing, fixing and patching SLS, and afterward, his professor asked, "Is there some way we can fix the install disks so that the new machines will have these fixes right away?"[30]

So Volkerding began improving SLS, and internally named his project Slackware, not wishing it to be taken too seriously.[30] By May 1993, though, the improvements were, "more than just cosmetic," and his friends convinced him to release it onto the internet. The first release proved so popular that the number of download requests crashed the system hosting it,[30] and Slackware would be, for several years, the de-facto Linux distribution.

Slackware 1.01, released 1993

Volkerding hadn't intended to produce a distribution,[30] but still maintains Slackware today.[31] The Slackware Linux Project is focused on producing "the most UNIX-like Linux distribution out there."[32]

Debian

At about the same time, Ian Murdoch was an undergraduate at Purdue University.[33] He saw distributions as essential to the future of Linux, but was dismayed by their quality.[34] The single-person and closed-room Cathedrals had consistently failed to produce quality products. More, the most popular distribution– SLS Linux– was, "...quite possibly the most bug-ridden and badly maintained Linux distribution available."[34]

Murdoch envisioned a distribution whose maintenance was community-driven— A modular operating system whose components were maintained by people with appropriate desires and specialities. He named the project Debian, a portmanteau of Ian and Debra, his wife:

"Debian Linux is a brand-new kind of Linux distribution. Rather than being developed by one isolated individual or group, as other distributions of Linux have been developed in the past, Debian is being developed openly in the spirit of Linux and GNU. The primary purpose of the Debian project is to finally create a distribution that lives up to the Linux name. Debian is being carefully and conscientiously put together and will be maintained and supported with similar care."[34]
Debian 4 "Etch", released 2007

Debian is one of the most respected distributions of our time. The quantity of software available through its repositories is unparalleled, and it is often used as a basis for derivate distributions.

Linux Mint

Aside:

Mint is one of several Debian derivatives.

My favorite distribution is Linux Mint, but then, I'm a simple guy. Mint is a characteristically professional operating system, and focuses on productivity— That means you'll be up and running before you know it.

Linux Mint 20.1 "Ulyssa", Cinnamon Edition, Released 2021

Mint is available with the above Cinnamon desktop environment; Windows users will feel right at home. I find it to be an attractive desktop, but it's only moderately customizable. Like many users, I want a desktop that stays out of my way, and Cinnamon does exactly that.

With version 19, Mint began shipping with Timeshift, a tool that automaticaly takes snapshots of the filesystem, so that users can restore the system to some earlier date.[35] Version 20 offers automatic updates.[36]

What sets Linux Mint apart from other distributions is that tools like Timeshift and Cinnamon are preinstalled and ready to use. When an example operating system is required, Mint will be used, but similar remarks apply to virtually any distribution, with a little perusing.
